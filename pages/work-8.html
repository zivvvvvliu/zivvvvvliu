ABSTRACT

In this paper, we introduce GazeLabel: a data labeling system with public displays (at bus stops, metro stations, shopping malls, etc.) using eye-tracking technology. With built-in cameras, the proposed system captures pedestrians’ gaze points on the screen, from which we can extract information and get a substantial amount of free labeled data. Based on this idea, we implemented a prototype eye-tracking data-labeling system for binary image classifications. A pilot study demonstrated the potential and practicability in a data labeling process using GazeLabel.

As the state of the art, Deep Learning is widely used in fields such as computer vision, voice recognition, and natural language processing. The learning process is driven by data, which needs to be labeled. therefore, data-labeling is a crucial step in deep learning projects. Currently, the most common approach to such task is “crowdsourcing", such as Amazon’s Mechanical Turk [1]. Yet, despite its speed and flexibility, crowdsourcing requires trivially detailed design, demands large amount of financial and human resources, and become difficult to manage due to variance in labelers’ abilities. The prevalence of public displays such as advertising screen panels, allows all sorts of information to be disseminated in the form of images and videos. Recent years, some research work has shown that gaze eye tracking could help us analyze the passers-by attention to enable users to interact with public displays. Such as EyeScout [4], GazeHorizon [5], AggreGaze [6]. In addition, eye-tracking technology has advanced rapidly. Especially deep learning method applied [3]. A single camera can achieve user’s eye tracking without calibration. Furthermore, an AI company Megvii (face++) has opened gaze estimation API [2] free for developers. We attempt to equip public displays with eye-tracking technology to solve the issues in crowdsourcing of data labeling. To reduce complexity and cost in crowdsourcing management, we propose to use public displays (at bus stops, metro stations, shopping malls, hospitals, etc.) as new data-labeling platforms, drawing support from pedestrians to voluntarily help us label data. We could setup a camera on the panel to capture the audience’s gaze points and apply a tailor-made user-interaction design to the screen, allowing the passing-by audience to help us label data almost unconsciously. The realization process of such eye-tracking data-labeling system is as follows. First, with the built-in camera of the screen panel, the system monitors and collects the audience’s gaze points with eye-tracking technology. The system then identifies an area on the screen that received greater attention (measured by gaze frequency and fixation duration on the area). The label decision represented by such area will be marked as “selected”. The decision from this panel could then be sent to the system’s backend server, which collects the decision votes from all panels and labels the data with the decision that received the most votes. Such approach captures user-inputs passively and single-directionally, so the traditional user-interaction based on mouse and keyboard cannot apply, where the next piece of data appears immediately upon completion of the previous. In the proposed system, only one data-piece is displayed over a specified amount of time, and all passerby would be attempting this same labeling task. The display duration of every data-piece is determined by frequency of passerby. The duration decreases as frequency of labeling attempts by passerby increases, and vice versa. Most urban cities have large numbers of advertising screen panels, indicating the system’s potential to label large amounts of data very quickly. Take Beijing as an example: there are at least millions of advertising screen panels in the city (Beijing has over 300 metro stations, over 700 hospitals, more than 200 large shopping malls, and tens of thousands of bus stops). Suppose an image labeling task requires 20 minutes to complete on the proposed system and assuming that it could work up to 18 hours per day, tens of millions of data-pieces could be process and labeled every single day. The scale of efficiency from this system could be very considerable and has the ability to provide a substantial amount of labeled data.
